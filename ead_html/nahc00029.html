Biographical/Historical note

Mr. Vincent began his automotive career in 1910 by working at the Hudson Motor Car Company as their chief engineer. Two years later, Alvin Macauley, the general manager of Packard, hired Mr. Vincent as Packard's chief engineer. At Packard, he developed the Twin Six, a 12-cylinder engine that was well received by the automobile industry. In 1915 he was appointed engineering vice president of Packard; a position he held until his retirement in 1949. He served on Packard's board of directors from 1945-1954. Mr. Vincent co-designed the World War I Liberty engine for which he received a colonelcy in the Army reserves. During World War II he designed the PT boat engine; he also contributed to the design of the Rolls Royce fighter plane engine. With the aid of Gar Wood, he designed racing engines for hydroplanes that won numerous Gold Cup records. Mr. Vincent was a long-standing member of the Society of Automotive Engineers, serving as president in 1920. He was a fellow of the American Society of Mechanical Engineers and of the Institute of Aeronautical Sciences. He was also a member of the Engineering Society of Detroit. He served as a director of the Automobile Association of America and of the Auto Club. He also served as president of the Automobile Club of Michigan. In 1929 the University of Michigan honored him with a Master of Engineering degree in recognition of his engineering contributions to the development of the automotive industry, and for the conspicuous service rendered to his country . His first wife, Clarinda Blood, died in June of 1943. He married his second wife, Rachel Robinson Hawes, in November of 1943: she died on May 10, 1974. There were no children from either marriage. Mr. Vincent died on April 20, 1962.

Scope and contents note
The 29 diaries cover the working years of one of America's foremost automotive engineers. Entries range from Mr. Vincent's thoughts on and descriptions of his engine design work, to remarks on his daily schedule. He even noted travel time, gas mileage and car performance when driving between his Grosse Pointe and northern Michigan homes. The diaries cover the height of his career at Packard when he developed Packard's notable single-eight engine and the Packard-built speedboat engines.
